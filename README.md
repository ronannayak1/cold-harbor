1. Architectural Paradigm: Bottom-Up Ensemble
Instead of employing a single, monolithic machine learning model to estimate the ultimate first-week performance of an album, the system splits consumption into separate, distinct behaviors. This approach honors the separate consumer dynamics that dictate different elements of a commercial rollout:  Streaming Consumption (streaming): Evaluates passive consumption (plays and platform streams) using the FIRST_WEEK_STREAMING_EQUIVALENT target column.  Digital Track Sales (song): Forecasts paid transaction behaviors (digital single downloads) using the FIRST_WEEK_SONG_SALE_EQUIVALENT target column.  Physical & Direct Product Purchases (product): Captures high-intent fan monetization (vinyl, CDs, and box sets) using the FIRST_WEEK_PRODUCT_SALES target column.  The system constructs the ultimate projection through Bottom-Up Reconciliation. It computes independent forecasts for the three components, applies explicit bounds to mitigate data anomalies, and adds them together:  $$\text{Final\_Total\_AE} = \text{Prediction}_{\text{streaming}} + \text{Prediction}_{\text{song}} + \text{Prediction}_{\text{product}}$$



2. Step-by-Step Data Engineering & Feature Extraction PipelineThe features powering the models are engineered through a two-phase architecture implemented in singles_archetypes.py.  Phase 1: Temporal Gating and Panel FilteringGating the Release Horizon:Meticulously cross-references weekly track-level datasets against a historical mapping table. To eliminate any potential forward-looking data leakage, a strict temporal cutoff removes any historical row where the single's tracking week end date is greater than or equal to the album's first sale date ($\text{WEEK\_END\_DATE} < \text{ALBUM\_FIRST\_SALE\_DATE}$).  Density Enforcement: The pipeline filters out short-lived or low-data releases by requiring a minimum of 2 observed tracking weeks ($\ge \text{MIN\_OBSERVED\_WEEKS}$) before a single qualifies for the mathematical modeling stage.  Phase 2: The 5-Step Meta-Archetyping EngineFor qualified pre-album singles, the data is channeled through a rigid sequence to transform unstructured time series into tabular features:  Step 1: Gamma Parameter Estimation & True Shape Normalization
The pipeline normalizes the volume curve of each individual track by its peak observed volume. It utilizes a non-linear optimization curve-fit (scipy.optimize.curve_fit) to map the rollout curve to a standardized gamma distribution function ($\gamma_{\text{norm}}$), outputting an alpha parameter (gamma_a) and peak time parameter (gamma_t_peak).
These curve shapes are then stacked into a single, combined time-grid for the album. Crucially, it maps two separate arrays:  $M_{\text{abs}}(t)$: The summation of raw, absolute volumes scaled by track peaks.  $M_{\text{norm}}(t)$: A unit-weight shape stacking where every track has an identical weight of $1.0$. This creates a completely dimensionless mathematical representation of the rollout shape.  Step 2: Cannibalization Ratio Calculation
When multiple singles overlap in time, the pipeline evaluates the residuals between a track's actual observed performance and its expected gamma decay curve at the precise moment a subsequent single drops. A negative aggregate value signifies cannibalization (one track eating the other's attention), while a positive value captures synergy/halo effects.  Step 3: Late-Promo Drop Bypass
Tracks that drop late in the cycle and fail to meet the 2-week observation threshold bypass the optimization curve fit entirely. Instead of breaking the pipeline, they are automatically matched to a rigid placeholder fallback template ($a = 1.5$, $t_{\text{peak}} = 1.0$) to guarantee stability.  Step 4: Expected Week-1 Carryover Integration
The pipeline integrates the absolute composite momentum function $M_{\text{abs}}(t)$ mathematically over the interval $[0, 1]$ (representing the exact week-1 release bounds) using a trapezoidal area-under-the-curve calculus (np.trapezoid). This quantifies the exact residual velocity entering the album's release week.  Step 5: Catalog Momentum Slope Extraction
By subtracting the active singles' volumes from the artist's total catalog baseline file, the pipeline isolates the historical baseline volume of the artist. It then computes a first-order linear regression slope (np.polyfit) across the historical time window from week $-8$ to week $-2$ relative to the album drop. This measures whether the artist's general catalog is expanding or contracting before the release.

This assigns the rollout profile to a distinct category, creating a mathematical abstraction of the release archetype. It pulls the artist's historical maximum performance, historical median performance, and historical single baseline volume. It then engineers relative growth interactions:  $$\text{momentum\_growth\_ratio} = \frac{\text{composite\_peak\_momentum}}{\text{historical\_single\_momentum}}$$



4. The Two-Stage Hurdle ArchitectureOnce the unified feature dataframe is constructed, each target executes an identical training and inference loop using a specialized Two-Stage Hurdle Architecture.  Stage 1: The Gatekeeper (LightGBM Classifier)The target-specific data evaluates its distribution and dynamically identifies its 75th percentile ($P_{75}$) threshold. A binary LGBMClassifier is trained via GroupKFold cross-validation (grouped by artist) to estimate the probability that the release will successfully clear that $P_{75}$ mark.  During inference, if the model assigns a probability $\le 0.50$ (CLASSIFIER_THRESHOLD), the release is rejected by the gatekeeper. It bypasses the secondary model entirely and receives a deterministic Fallback Prediction equal to its calculated single carryover volume plus a fallback baseline constant.  Stage 2: The Magnitude Extrapolator (VotingRegressor)If the album passes the Stage 1 gatekeeper, it is passed to a high-tier regressor trained strictly on historical releases that cleared the $P_{75}$ boundary. To eliminate the interpolation ceiling common in pure decision-tree structures, the regressor is deployed as an ensemble VotingRegressor containing two models:  LightGBM Regressor (LGBMRegressor): Learns non-linear feature interactions and localized structures across the dense mid-tier data.  Ridge Regression Pipeline (Ridge): Standardizes numeric features and computes a regularized linear regression. Because it utilizes traditional multiplication coefficients, it can extrapolate on the heavy tail, scaling predictions up for massive superstar rollouts unconstrained by past historical training bounds.  The entire regression target is log-transformed ($\log(1+x)$) during training to handle the volatility of scale, and converted back via an exponential inverse (np.expm1) during live inference.



5. System Shortcomings & Pipeline BlindspotsDespite its architectural rigor, this forecasting framework carries explicit blindspots that require careful monitoring:⚠️ Vulnerabilities and Structural GapsRelative Volatility Sensitivity (The Sophomore Slump Trap): Because the pipeline relies heavily on the momentum_growth_ratio, an artist who is coming off a record-breaking, viral debut album will face a math penalty if their new singles perform normally. A 40% drop in relative single momentum will cause the Ridge component to aggressively drag down the baseline projection, forcing a reliance on custom post-inference safety nets like the SUPERSTAR_FLOOR_RATIO.  Blindness to Physical Variant Strategy: While the product pipeline leverages historical statistics to gauge general physical intent, it is completely blind to current execution tactics. The model cannot read how many vinyl variants, colored editions, or signed CDs are manufactured or available for pre-order. It assumes the supply strategy matches the historical baseline, which can lead to over-prediction if supply is heavily restricted.Debut Artist "Cold Start" in Physical Domains: For breakout debut artists with an absolute zero history of product sales, the pipeline cannot establish a historical baseline or calculate a growth ratio. The model is forced to allocate them entirely based on their shape cluster probability, meaning a breakout digital streaming star could receive an inflated or unpredictable physical product prediction unless hard-coded caps (such as the 5% fallback constraint) suppress the output.  Asymmetric Data Dependencies: The pipeline relies on multiple sub-directories (data/sales/, data/songs/, and artist_ae_baseline.parquet) matching perfectly on string keys like DISPLAY_ARTIST. Slight differences in formatting or spelling can disrupt the pipeline, causing artists to fall back to the "debut" pipeline incorrectly.
